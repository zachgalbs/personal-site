Productivity

Systems for focus in an age of distraction

Productivity is not about doing more things. It's about doing the right things with sustained attention. In our hyperconnected world, the scarcest resource is not time but focused attention. This page documents experiments in attention management, distraction elimination, and the design of sustainable work practices.

The approach here differs from typical productivity advice in several ways: it prioritizes attention over time, systems over motivation, and sustainability over optimization. The goal is not peak performance but consistent, meaningful progress.

The Distraction Problem

Modern knowledge work operates in an environment designed to fragment attention. Email, notifications, open-plan offices, and the constant availability of information create what Linda Stone calls "continuous partial attention" — a state where we're always monitoring but never fully engaged.

Types of Distraction

Understanding distraction requires distinguishing between its various forms:

  • External interruptions: Email, messages, phone calls, colleagues
  • Internal distractions: Wandering thoughts, anxiety, task-switching impulses
  • Pseudo-work: Busy work that feels productive but doesn't advance meaningful goals
  • Context switching: The cognitive overhead of moving between different types of tasks

Each type requires different countermeasures. External interruptions can be managed through environmental design, while internal distractions often require mindfulness practices and better work structure.

Attention Management Framework

Rather than managing time, I focus on managing attention across four dimensions:

Depth (Focused vs. Diffuse)

Deep work requires sustained focus on cognitively demanding tasks. This state is incompatible with multitasking or frequent interruptions. I protect this mode through:

  • Time blocking: 90-120 minute sessions of uninterrupted work
  • Environmental design: Dedicated space with minimal visual distractions
  • Digital minimalism: Phone in another room, notifications disabled
  • Ritual: Consistent start/end routines to signal mode transitions

Diffuse attention is equally important for creativity and problem-solving. Walking, showering, or simple manual tasks often trigger insights that focused thinking cannot reach.

Direction (Reactive vs. Proactive)

Reactive attention responds to external stimuli — email, requests, urgencies. While necessary, reactive mode can consume entire days without advancing important goals.

Proactive attention is self-directed toward chosen priorities. I protect proactive time by:

  • Starting each day with proactive work before checking email
  • Batch processing reactive tasks at designated times
  • Using "capture" systems to defer non-urgent reactive items
  • Regular review cycles to ensure reactive work serves larger goals
Scope (Narrow vs. Broad)

Narrow attention focuses on specific tasks or problems. Broad attention maintains awareness of larger context, connections, and priorities.

Both are necessary. I cultivate broad attention through:

  • Weekly reviews to maintain perspective on goals and progress
  • Regular breaks to step back from immediate tasks
  • Connecting current work to larger purposes and values
  • Periodic "zoom out" sessions to reassess priorities
Energy (High vs. Low)

Attention quality varies with energy levels. High-energy attention is capable of sustained focus and complex reasoning. Low-energy attention is better suited to routine tasks.

I match tasks to energy levels by:

  • Tracking energy patterns to identify peak performance times
  • Scheduling demanding work during high-energy periods
  • Maintaining a "low-energy task list" for tired moments
  • Protecting energy through sleep, exercise, and stress management

Practical Systems

Daily Structure

My daily routine optimizes for sustained attention rather than maximal output:

Time Activity Attention Mode Purpose
6:00-7:00 Morning routine Diffuse Prepare mind for focused work
7:00-9:30 Deep work block Focused/Proactive Most important creative work
9:30-10:00 Break/movement Diffuse Cognitive recovery
10:00-12:00 Collaborative work Focused/Reactive Meetings, communication
12:00-13:00 Lunch/walk Diffuse Physical and mental break
13:00-15:00 Administrative tasks Reactive Email, planning, organizing
15:00-17:00 Learning/research Broad/Proactive Skill development, exploration

This structure protects high-quality attention for important work while accommodating necessary reactive tasks. The specific times matter less than the principle of matching work types to attention modes.

Environmental Design

Physical and digital environments significantly impact attention quality. Key principles:

  • Reduce visual clutter: Clear surfaces, minimal decorations, organized spaces
  • Control lighting: Natural light when possible, adjustable artificial lighting
  • Manage sound: Quiet spaces for focused work, background noise for routine tasks
  • Optimize ergonomics: Comfortable seating, proper screen height, accessible tools
  • Digital minimalism: Close unnecessary applications, disable non-essential notifications

Capture Systems

A reliable capture system prevents thoughts and tasks from occupying working memory. I use:

  • Inbox: Single location for all incoming items (physical notebook)
  • Processing ritual: Daily review and categorization of captured items
  • Action lists: Next actions organized by context and energy level
  • Reference system: Easy retrieval of information and ideas

Experiments and Results

Phone Separation

Hypothesis: Physical separation from smartphone would reduce distraction and improve focus quality.

Method: For 30 days, kept phone in another room during work hours. Checked messages only at designated times (10am, 2pm, 6pm).

Results:

  • Reduced urge to check phone within first week
  • Improved ability to sustain attention on single tasks
  • Increased anxiety about missing urgent messages (first week only)
  • Better completion rates for deep work sessions

Conclusion: Physical separation is more effective than relying on willpower to resist checking. The inconvenience of retrieval creates enough friction to break habitual reaching.

Notification Audit

Hypothesis: Most notifications are unnecessary and can be eliminated without missing important information.

Method: Cataloged all notifications across devices and applications. Disabled all non-essential notifications. Tracked what (if anything) was missed over 60 days.

Results:

  • Reduced notifications from ~50 to 3 per day
  • Missed exactly zero important communications
  • Improved focus during work sessions
  • Less fragmented attention throughout the day

Conclusion: The vast majority of notifications are not urgent or important. Default settings are designed for engagement, not productivity.

Time-Blocking vs. Task Lists

Hypothesis: Scheduling specific times for tasks (time-blocking) would be more effective than maintaining flexible task lists.

Method: Alternated between time-blocking and task-list approaches for 8 weeks each. Measured task completion rates and subjective satisfaction.

Results:

  • Time-blocking: Higher completion rate for important tasks, lower flexibility
  • Task lists: Better adaptation to changing priorities, lower completion rates
  • Hybrid approach emerged: time-block for deep work, task lists for reactive work

Conclusion: Different approaches suit different types of work. The key is matching the planning method to the work's characteristics.

Current Principles

Based on ongoing experimentation, these principles currently guide my productivity approach:

  1. Attention is the constraint: Optimize for attention quality, not time quantity
  2. Systems over willpower: Design environments and processes that make good choices easier
  3. Sustainable pace: Optimize for consistency over peak performance
  4. Energy management: Match work types to energy levels and attention modes
  5. Regular experimentation: Continuously test and refine approaches
  6. Context matters: What works varies by person, work type, and life circumstances

The most important insight: productivity is not about finding the perfect system but about developing the skill of system design and refinement. The specific tactics matter less than the ability to observe, experiment, and adapt.
