About

Purpose, approach, and principles

This site documents ongoing experiments in systematic thinking applied to various domains of life and work. Rather than offering definitive answers or universal solutions, it presents tested approaches, failed experiments, and the reasoning behind current methods.

The content here represents work in progress, not finished conclusions. Ideas are tested, refined, and sometimes discarded as new information emerges. The goal is contributing useful data points to the collective understanding of how to build better systems for thinking, creating, and living.

Motivation

The modern world presents us with unprecedented complexity and choice. Traditional approaches to work, creativity, and decision-making often prove inadequate for navigating this landscape. At the same time, we have access to tools and knowledge that enable new forms of systematic thinking.

This site explores the gap between theoretical knowledge and practical application. It's one thing to read about productivity systems or brewing techniques; it's another to implement them consistently in real circumstances with real constraints.

Core Questions

  • How can systematic approaches enhance rather than replace human judgment?
  • What works in practice versus what works in theory?
  • How do we balance optimization with sustainability?
  • When should we follow systems and when should we abandon them?
  • How can we maintain curiosity while building reliable processes?

These questions don't have universal answers, but exploring them in specific contexts reveals patterns and principles that often generalize to other domains.

Approach

The methodology here draws from several traditions:

Scientific Method

  • Form hypotheses about what might work
  • Design experiments to test specific claims
  • Measure results objectively when possible
  • Document both successes and failures
  • Iterate based on evidence

Engineering Mindset

  • Optimize for specific constraints and requirements
  • Consider trade-offs and limitations
  • Build for reliability and maintainability
  • Focus on what works, not what's theoretically optimal
  • Embrace practical compromises

Craftsperson Tradition

  • Develop skill through deliberate practice
  • Understand tools and materials deeply
  • Value process as much as outcome
  • Seek mastery through repetition and refinement
  • Learn from both success and failure

"The expert in anything was once a beginner who refused to give up."

— Helen Hayes

Content Philosophy

Specificity Over Generality

Rather than broad principles, the focus is on specific implementations tested in specific contexts. General advice is often useless; specific examples can be adapted to different circumstances.

Process Over Product

The thinking behind decisions is often more valuable than the decisions themselves. By documenting reasoning, assumptions, and constraints, others can evaluate whether similar approaches might work in their contexts.

Honest Reporting

Failed experiments and abandoned approaches are documented alongside successes. This provides a more complete picture and helps others avoid repeating ineffective strategies.

Practical Focus

All content is grounded in actual practice. Theoretical frameworks are included only when they prove useful for guiding real-world decisions and actions.

Areas of Focus

Current explorations span several domains:

Productivity Systems

Attention management, distraction elimination, and workflow design. The focus is on sustainable practices that enhance rather than complicate daily work.

Key interests: deep work protection, capture systems, energy management, context switching reduction.

Musical Analysis

Understanding why certain music is compelling through compositional analysis, technology exploration, and cross-domain application of musical principles.

Key interests: generative composition, microtonal systems, spatial audio, emotional architecture.

Coffee Science

Systematic approach to brewing better coffee through understanding extraction chemistry, equipment analysis, and methodical experimentation.

Key interests: grind optimization, water chemistry, brewing method comparison, freshness management.

System Design

Broader explorations of how to build reliable, maintainable systems for knowledge work, creative practice, and decision-making.

Key interests: automation boundaries, human-computer collaboration, sustainable optimization.

Technical Details

Site Design

This site is built using pure HTML, CSS, and minimal JavaScript. The design prioritizes:

  • Readability: Typography optimized for sustained reading
  • Performance: Fast loading, minimal dependencies
  • Accessibility: Works without JavaScript, screen reader friendly
  • Sustainability: Low energy consumption, minimal server requirements
  • Longevity: Simple technologies that remain stable over time

The visual design draws inspiration from academic papers, technical documentation, and book typography. The monochrome palette eliminates visual distraction while the generous whitespace provides breathing room for complex ideas.

Content Management

Content is maintained in plain HTML files with minimal structure. This approach:

  • Reduces complexity and potential failure modes
  • Enables easy backup and version control
  • Allows direct editing without specialized tools
  • Ensures content remains accessible regardless of platform changes
  • Supports fast, reliable deployment

Sidenotes System

The sidenotes feature is implemented using progressive enhancement:

  • Works without JavaScript (falls back to inline notes)
  • Positions notes in margin on wide screens
  • Collapses to expandable inline notes on mobile
  • Maintains readability across all device sizes

This technical approach reflects the broader philosophy: start with solid basics, then enhance without breaking core functionality.

Contact

This site represents ongoing work rather than finished conclusions. If you've found errors, have suggestions for improvements, or want to share related experiences, feedback is welcome.

The most useful feedback tends to be:

  • Specific corrections to factual claims
  • Alternative approaches that have worked in similar contexts
  • Questions that reveal unclear explanations
  • Results from replicating described experiments
  • Connections to related work or resources

Contact information and current status can be found at the bottom of each page. Response time varies based on current project commitments, but substantive feedback typically receives a thoughtful reply.
